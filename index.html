I'm an interdisciplinary researcher trying to bridge the fields of astrophysics and machine learning. I work as a Senior Research Associate at the Center for Cosmology and Particle Physics (New York University) where I started in 2019.

Research overview

My research interests are astrophysics as well as machine learning. Sometimes these interests overlap - sometimes they don't.
Astrophysics: I focus on stars before, during, and after explosion (exploding stars are also known as supernovae). In my supernova research, I'm particularly intrigued by hydrogen-poor supernovae (Type Ia and Type Ib/c): Why do some explosions in the Universe not show the most abundant element? I approach this question from two sides: 1) What was their life like before the explosion? 2) How can we reconstruct the explosion from observations? For the first topic, I search for former companions that have survived the explosion and can be seen at the site of former explosions (so called supernova remnants ?? insert picture here ??) I am trying to reconstruct the explosion by using the radiative transfer code TARDIS developed by me and my team. I'm not only interested in explosion. I also am interested in understanding the composition of stars. In particular, I want to understand the unusual elemental abundances in the star cluster around the Milky Way's supermassive black hole.
Machine Learning and Natural Language processing Finally, I am working with the Science Data Group at ESO to think about novell ways to extract measurements from raw data. In particular, I am developing a method to use all available information using a statistical framework when extracting spectra from X-Shooter data.
Research Philosophy All of this research is tied together with an interest in numerical methods, novel algorithms and the idea of open software as well as open collaboration.
